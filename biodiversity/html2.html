Mango Tree

Mango trees are deep-rooted, symmetrical evergreens that attain heights of 90 feet and widths of 80 feet.
Mango trees have simple alternate lanceolate leaves that are 12 to 16 inches in length and yellow-green, purple, or copper in color when young.
Mature leaves are leathery, glossy, and deep green in color. They also originate from Southeast Asia.

Lily

Lilies typically feature 6 tepal flowers in a variety of shapes (trumpet, funnel, cup, bell, bowl or flat), sometimes nodding,
sometimes with reflexed petals, atop stiff, unbranched stems (1-8 feet tall) clothed with linear to elliptic leaves. Flowers are often fragrant and come
in a broad range of colors except blue. They also originate from Central and East Asian.

Cactus

Cacti are plants that have succulent stems, pads or branches with scales and spines instead of leaves. Cactus pads are
actually modified stems with a waxy coating. The prickly spines are modified leaves that break up
winds that blow across pad surfaces, and help shade the stem. They also originate from America.

Bamboo

Bamboo is a complex, woody stemmed perennial grass belonging to family Poaceae. The portion of bamboo below the
ground level is called rhizome, which forms the vital source for its perennial growth. Roots are produced by the rhizome and
also extend up to the lower nodes of the stems that are below the soil. They also originate from China.

Dandelion

Dandelion is a hardy perennial that can grow to a height of nearly 12 inches. The plants have deeply-notched, toothy, spatula-like
leaves that are shiny and hairless. Dandelion stems are capped by bright yellow flowers. The grooved
leaves funnel rain to the root. They also originate from Eurasia or Europe and Asia.

Spider

Spiders (order Araneae) are air-breathing arthropods that have eight limbs, chelicerae with fangs generally able to inject venom, and
spinnerets that extrude silk. They are the largest order of arachnids and rank seventh in total species diversity among all orders
of organisms. Spiders are found all over the world, and they evolved over millions of years, so they don't have a specific origin country.

Wasp

Like all insects, wasps have three pairs of legs, antennae, and three body segments called the head, thorax and abdomen. Wasps
can be distinguished from bees by their pointed lower abdomen and a narrow waist called a petiole, between the abdomen and thorax. Wasp size varies among species.
Just like spiders, wasps also originate from all around the world. Wasps are found globally and evolved over time, so they donâ€™t originate from a single country.

Bird

Birds are warm-blooded animals with feathers, beaks, and wings. They lay eggs and are found in many habitats around the world. While many
can fly, some, like ostriches and penguins, cannot. Birds play important roles in nature, such as pollinating plants and controlling pests.
Birds also evolved over millions of years and don't originate from a single country.

Dragonfly

Dragonfly species (Anisoptera) are characterized by long bodies with two narrow pairs of intricately veined, membranous wings that,
while generally transparent, may have coloured markings. Unlike damselflies, the front and rear wing pairs are shaped differently.
Dragonflies actually don't have a specific origin country since they evolved over millions of years and are now found globally.

Butterfly

Butterflies have six jointed legs, a pair of antennae and three body parts called a head, thorax (chest), and abdomen (tail end). The four wings
and the six legs of a butterfly are attached to its thorax. Special sensors on a butterfly or moths' feet allow the insect to taste their food.
Butterflies don't actually have an origin country, but they did evolve around 50 to 100 million years ago, with their ancestors likely originating in tropical regions.
